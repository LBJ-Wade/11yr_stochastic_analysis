The NANOGrav 11-year Data Set

Pulsar-timing Constraints On The Stochastic Gravitational-wave Background

by The NANOGrav Collaboration
Corresponding author: Stephen R. Taylor

We search for a gravitational-wave background (GWB) in the newly released 11-year dataset from the North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves (NANOGrav). While we find no significant evidence for a GWB, we place constraints on a GWB from a population of inspiraling supermassive black hole binaries, from a network of decaying cosmic strings, and from a primordial GWB. For the first time, we find that the GWB upper limits and detection statistics are sensitive to the Solar System ephemeris (SSE) model used, and that SSE errors can mimic a GWB signal. We developed and implemented a novel approach that bridges SSE differences, producing the first pulsar-timing constraints that are robust against SSE uncertainties.

Searching For Gravitational-waves Using Pulsars

Image credit: NRAO

  • Precision timing of millisecond pulsars over years and decades allows us to build models of radio-pulse arrival times with incredible accuracy. These models account for the pulsar's spin period, how it's spin period is changing, dispersion of radio pulses in the interstellar medium, and many other effects.

  • A gravitational-wave (GW) propagating between the Earth and a pulsar will squeeze and stretch the intervening space-time. This causes an advance or delay of the pulse arrival times with respect to our model's predictions.

  • A random background of GWs will lead to apparent noise in the pulsar arrival times, with power predominately at low frequencies. Such a background could come from many inspiraling supermassive black-hole binaries (SMBHBs), a network of decaying cosmic strings, or primordial space-time fluctuations.

  • But this background affects all pulsars in the Milky Way in a predictably correlated way. We leverage this signature, correlating data from our large arrays of pulsars to search for the common influence of GWs, and intepreting the results in terms of potential sources.

11-year Data Set

  • NANOGrav's latest data release contains radio-pulse times of arrival (TOAs) and timing-models for 45 millisecond pulsars.

  • The observations span 11.4 years, from July 30th 2005 to December 31st 2015. The pulsar with the longest baseline is J1744-1134, which has 11.37 years of observations. The pulsar with the largest data volume is J1713+0747, with 27,571 TOAs.

  • Observations were carried out using the 100-m Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope (GBT) of the Green Bank Observatory, and the 305-m William E. Gordon Telescope (Arecibo) of Arecibo Observatory.

  • In the sky-map shown here, the area of each circle is indicative of the number of TOAs that a pulsar has, while the color scale indicates the observational baseline. The 34 pulsars with baselines greater than 3 years have solid red edges. We use only these 34 in our searches for a background signal of gravitational waves.

Ephemeris dependence of results

  • Our fiducial analysis treats the power spectrum of timing deviations caused by a GWB as a power-law, with an amplitude (calibrated to a frequency of 1/year) and a spectral index. A GWB from a population of supermassive black-hole binaries (SMBHBs) has an index of 4.33.

  • Upper limits (shown here at 95% credibility) and signal-vs-noise Bayes factors depend strongly on which model of the Solar-System Ephemeris (SSE) we used. We tested a variety of models from the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), and also some INPOP models. Bayes factors vary by over an order of magnitude (from non-detection to promising feature) depending on the SSE. Hence, systematic errors in the SSE can mimic a GW signal, giving us spurious results.

  • Most errors arise from uncertainties on Jupiter's orbital properties, and the correlated influence of Uranus and Neptune's uncertainties on Jupiter. These make it hard to compute the center of the Solar-System to the precision and accuracy needed for pulsar-timing GW detection.

  • We have developed a scheme (known as ''BayesEphem'') to mitigate these SSE errors, which is described below.

BayesEphem

  • Our modeling scheme begins with a baseline estimate of the SSE (one of the JPL DE- models, or other).

  • We then construct an 11-parameter model that perturbs the position of the Solar-System barycenter (SSB). This corresponds to a drift-rate of the Earth's orbit about the ecliptic z-axis, mass perturbations to the gas giants, and linear corrections to Jupiter's orbital elements (e.g. eccentricity, semi-major axis, etc.). Jupiter's orbit is the dominant influence.

  • Regardless of which baseline SSE model we use, our correction scheme (''BayesEphem'') bridges the systematic differences between them, giving consistent GWB upper limits and detection statistics. This is shown in the image here.

  • Our fiducial GWB amplitude upper limit is 1.45e-15 (when inter-pulsar correlations are modeled), and 1.34e-15 (when correlations are not modeled). The signal-vs-noise Bayes factors mildly disfavor a GWB signal, but the value is very weak. There is no evidence for the expected inter-pulsar correlation signature from GWs.

  • Previous results from NANOGrav (and other PTAs) must be revised in light of this new modeling scheme. NANOGrav's 9-year Data Set upper limit is revised from 1.5e-15 under DE421 to 2.67e-15 (without correlations) and 2.91e-15 (with correlations).

Supermassive Black-hole Binaries

Image: (Top-) Bayesian joint probability distribution for galactic-core stellar density (y-axs) and SMBHB eccentricity (x-axis), at several fixed values of the y-intercept of the BH-bulge mass relationship. (Bottom-) Translation of probability distribution to strain spectra. Larger y-intercept values give larger spectral amplitudes, needing stronger environmental driving to attenuate the spectrum at low frequencies and thereby avoid inconsistencies with the data.

  • A GWB can be created from the incoherent superposition of GW signals from inspiraling SMBHBs throughout the Universe. These binaries form as massive galaxies merge, and their resident SMBHs sink together to form a bound system.

  • The fiducial characteristic strain spectrum from such a SMBHB population is a power-law with a -2/3 spectral index. But many processes can influence the amplitude and shape of this spectrum.

  • SMBHBs can remain strongly coupled to their galactic environments even at frequencies in the PTA sensitivity band. This causes the spectrum to deviate from a power-law, creating a turnover. Binary eccentricity can also cause spectral attenutation, as power is distributed to higher orbital frequency harmonics.

  • We trained a Gaussian Process to interpolate over spectra from a grid of binary population simulations. This gave us joint constraints on BH-galaxy scaling relationships, galactic-core stellar densities, and typical SMBHB eccentricities. This modeling scheme can be trivially expanded to include many other dynamical and demographic properties.

Cosmic Strings

  • Cosmic strings are topological defects in space-time that could have formed when the early Universe underwent a phase transition. Networks of these long strings can intersect, chopping off small loops that vibrate relativistically, and decay via GW emission. Higher string tensions (x-axis in the image here) give stronger GW emission.

  • Field-Theory strings have a definite probability of intersecting when they inhabit the same space. But String-Theory strings must also intersect in all higher compactified spatial dimensions, such that intersection probabilities (y-axis in the image here) can be less than one.

  • We trained a Gaussian Process to interpolate over spectra from the most up-to-date cosmic-string simulations, allowing us to directly constrain string tension at different intersection probabilities. Our modeling scheme and constraints improve on the NANOGrav 9-year and most recent EPTA results.

  • Our upper limit on the cosmic-string tension (at an intersection probability of one) is 5.3e-11. At this level, cosmic-string signatures are unlikely to be seen in the CMB power spectrum or through other means. PTAs continue to be the best tool for probing cosmic strings.
