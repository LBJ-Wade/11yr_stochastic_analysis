The NANOGrav 11-year Data Set

Pulsar-timing Constraints On The Stochastic Gravitational-wave Background

by The NANOGrav Collaboration
Corresponding author: Stephen R. Taylor

We search for a gravitational-wave background (GWB) in the newly released 11-year dataset from the North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves (NANOGrav). While we find no significant evidence for a GWB, we place constraints on a GWB from a population of inspiraling supermassive black hole binaries, from a network of decaying cosmic strings, and from a primordial GWB. For the first time, we find that the GWB upper limits and detection statistics are sensitive to the Solar System ephemeris (SSE) model used, and that SSE errors can mimic a GWB signal. We developed and implemented a novel approach that bridges SSE differences, producing the first pulsar-timing constraints that are robust against SSE uncertainties.

11-year Data Set

  • NANOGrav's latest data release contains radio-pulse times of arrival (TOAs) and timing-models for 45 millisecond pulsars.

  • The observations span 11.4 years, from July 30th 2005 to December 31st 2105. The pulsar with the longest baseline is J1744-1134, which has 11.37 years of observations. The pulsar with the largest data volume is J1713+0747, with 27,571 TOAs.

  • Observations were carried out using the 100-m Robert C. Byrd Green Bank Telescope (GBT) of the Green Bank Observatory, and the 305-m William E. Gordon Telescope (Arecibo) of Arecibo Observatory.

  • In the sky-map shown here, the area of each circle is indicative of the number of TOAs that a pulsar has, while the color scale indicates the observational baseline. The 34 pulsars with baselines greater than 3 years have solid red edges. We use only these 34 in our searches for a background signal of gravitational waves.

Ephemeris dependence of results

  • Our fiducial analysis treats the power spectrum of timing deviations caused by a GWB as a power-law, with an amplitude (calibrated to a frequency of 1/year) and a spectral index. A GWB from a population of supermassive black-hole binaries (SMBHBs) has an index of 4.33.

  • Upper limits (and signal-vs-noise Bayes factors) depend strongly on which model of the Solar-System Ephemeris (SSE) we used. We tested a variety of models from the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and also some INPOP models.

  • The variation in GW strain-amplitude upper limits is shown in the image here. For a SMBHB background, the characteristic strain upper limit is 1.45e-15 (when inter-pulsar correlations are modeled), and 1.34e-15 (when correlations are not modeled). Signal-vs-noise Bayes factors vary by over an order of magnitude depending on the SSE. Hence, systematic errors in the SSE can mimic a GW signal, giving us spurious results.
